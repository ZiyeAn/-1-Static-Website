Stem

Brief Intro to Stem

The stem (also called the stipe) of a mushroom is the supporting structure that holds the cap above the ground. While not all mushrooms have stems (e.g., some polypores and crust fungi), the stem is a critical feature for identifying and distinguishing different species in mushrooms that do have one.
The evolutionary benefit of a stipe is generally considered to be in mediating spore dispersal. An elevated mushroom will more easily release its spores into wind currents or onto passing animals. Nevertheless, many mushrooms do not have stipes, including cup fungi, puffballs, earthstars, some polypores, jelly fungi, ergots, and smuts.

Stem Shape

Cylindrical:
• Most common type, straight and uniform in thickness.

Clavate:
• Club-shaped, thicker at the base.

Tapered:
Narrowing either at the base or at the top, forming a cone-like shape.

Bulbous:
• Swollen or bulb-like at the base.

Rooting:
The stem extends deeply into the substrate, sometimes resembling a root.
